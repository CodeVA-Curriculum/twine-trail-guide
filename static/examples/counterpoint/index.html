In the early hours of August 22, 1831, an enslaved man named Nat Turner led more than fifty followers in a bloody revolt in Southampton, Virginia. They killed almost 60 white people, mostly women and children. The local authorities stopped the rebellion by the next day. They captured or killed most of the rebels, although Turner himself managed to avoid capture for sixty days. On November 5, Nat Turner was tried in the Southampton County Court and sentenced to execution. He was killed on November 11. After the rebellion, white mobs would murder close to 200 black people, many of whom had nothing to do with the rebellion. In addition, slaves as far away as North Carolina were accused of having a connection with the insurrection, and were subsequently tried and executed. The Fugitive Slave Law of 1850 was part of the Compromise of 1850. This law required the United States government to help southern slave holders in capturing those African-Americans who ranaway to freedom. Under the United States Constitution, slave holders had the "right" to reclaim slaves who ran away to free states. With the Fugitive Slave Law of 1850, the national government now had to assist the slave holders in capturing runaways or "fugitives." The Fugitive Slave Law clearly benefited the slave holders. Anyone caught hiding or assisting runaways would face harsh punishments. As a result of this law, thousands of African Americans fled to Canada. Some people who had been free for their entire lives left the country.
